Lorentz Invariance

Overview

This page re-examines the framework’s Lorentz invariance derivation through Spacetime Algebra (the Clifford algebra Cl(1,3)), where boosts and rotations are unified into a single type of transformation called a rotor.

What changes. In the standard derivation, rotations and boosts are introduced separately — three rotation generators JiJ_iJi​ and three boost generators KiK_iKi​ — then assembled into the Lorentz group through their commutation relations. In Spacetime Algebra, both are special cases of one formula: R=e−Bθ/2R = e^{-B\theta/2}R=e−Bθ/2, where BBB is an oriented plane (a bivector). If BBB is a spatial plane, the rotor generates a rotation; if BBB involves the time direction, it generates a boost. The difference between rotating and boosting is the signature of the plane, nothing more. This collapses two derivation steps into one and makes Thomas precession — a notoriously subtle effect where successive boosts in different directions produce an unexpected rotation — fall out of a single multiplication.

What stays the same. The physics is identical — time dilation, length contraction, the speed limit, and the Poincaré group are all reproduced exactly. The derivation chain (loop closure →\to→ Minkowski geometry →\to→ Lorentz transformations) is unchanged. What GA provides is structural clarity: why boosts and rotations are related, why composing boosts gives a rotation, and why the speed limit exists all become visible properties of the algebra.

Key insights for non-experts:

  • One formula for all Lorentz transformations. Instead of separate treatments of rotations and boosts, both are R=e−Bθ/2R = e^{-B\theta/2}R=e−Bθ/2 for different choices of plane BBB. The unification is algebraic, not notational.
  • Thomas precession in one line. Composing a boost along xxx with a boost along yyy produces a spatial rotation because the product of two timelike planes is a spacelike plane: e02e01=e12e_{02}e_{01} = e_{12}e02​e01​=e12​. This three-symbol equation replaces a page-long computation.
  • The double cover is built in. A 2π2\pi2π rotation gives R=−1R = -1R=−1, not +1+1+1 — you need 4π4\pi4π to get back to where you started. This sign flip, which is the algebraic root of fermion behavior, is visible in the algebra without constructing SU(2)\mathrm{SU}(2)SU(2) separately.
  • The speed limit from topology. Rotations trace closed loops (periodic, like a clock); boosts trace open curves (unbounded, like an accelerator). The speed limit is the statement that the boost direction never closes — rapidity goes to infinity while velocity approaches ccc.

Assessment: What GA Adds

Genuine simplifications:

  1. Unified treatment of boosts and rotations. The standard derivation needs separate constructions for rotations (Step 4, JiJ_iJi​ generators) and boosts (Step 5, KiK_iKi​ generators and the 4×44 \times 44×4 matrix). STA reduces this to: every continuous Lorentz transformation is R=e−Bθ/2R = e^{-B\theta/2}R=e−Bθ/2 for some bivector BBB. Two derivation steps collapse to one.

  2. Commutation relations for free. The Lorentz algebra commutation relations (Theorem 4.2 of the target) follow from three lines of bivector multiplication (Proposition 2.2). No matrix representations needed.

  3. Thomas precession is automatic. The standard treatment requires a subtle computation. In STA, the Thomas-Wigner rotation falls out of a single product e02e01=e12e_{02}e_{01} = e_{12}e02​e01​=e12​ (Theorem 5.1). This partially resolves Gap 2 of the target derivation.

  4. Double cover is intrinsic. The distinction between Spin+(1,3)\mathrm{Spin}^+(1,3)Spin+(1,3) and SO+(1,3)\mathrm{SO}^+(1,3)SO+(1,3) is algebraically visible: R(2π)=−1≠+1R(2\pi) = -1 \neq +1R(2π)=−1=+1. No separate construction of SL(2,C)\mathrm{SL}(2, \mathbb{C})SL(2,C) needed.

Genuine insights:

  1. Signature from bivector squares. The B2=−1B^2 = -1B2=−1 vs. B2=+1B^2 = +1B2=+1 dichotomy is the distinction between rotation and boost. The indefinite signature of spacetime appears as a single algebraic fact about bivectors.

  2. Loop closure connection. The rotor one-parameter subgroup for spatial rotations is a U(1)U(1)U(1) loop that mirrors Axiom 3’s phase dynamics (Proposition 8.2). This structural parallel — rotor closure = loop closure — connects the Lorentz group to the framework’s axioms at a deeper level than the standard treatment.

  3. Speed limit from compactness. Rotations close (compact U(1)U(1)U(1)), boosts do not (non-compact R\mathbb{R}R). The speed limit is the statement that the boost subgroup is non-compact (Proposition 8.3).

Not a genuine simplification (just notation):

  • Time dilation and length contraction (Step 4 above) use rotor computations that are no shorter than the standard Minkowski interval argument. The GA version is elegant but not simpler.
  • The Poincaré group construction (Step 9) is essentially unchanged from the standard treatment.
