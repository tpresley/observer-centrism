12. Quantum Mechanics: The Observer-Centric Interpretation

Quantum Mechanics: The Observer-Centric Interpretation

The measurement problem — why quantum experiments yield definite outcomes from indefinite quantum states — has occupied physicists and philosophers since the 1920s. The major interpretations (Copenhagen, Many Worlds, Bohmian mechanics, GRW objective collapse, relational quantum mechanics) each resolve some aspects of the problem while inheriting others. Observer-Centrism offers a resolution that derives rather than postulates the key features: the Born rule, the preferred basis, the single-outcome structure, and the observer-dependence of quantum states.

12.1 The Wavefunction as Relational Coherence State

The wavefunction ψ\psiψ of a quantum system is, in the framework, the coherence state of that system relative to the coherence domain of a specific observer. It is not purely epistemic (merely a calculational tool) nor straightforwardly ontic (a physical field in space). It is relationally real: it describes a genuine conserved structure — the coherence relationships between the system and the observer’s network — that is real relative to a specific coherence domain.

Different observers with different coherence domains may assign different wavefunctions to the same system, and both may be correct — relative to their respective domains. This is not subjective uncertainty about an objective state. It is the structural consequence of the framework’s relational ontology: quantum states are facts relative to coherence domains, not facts simpliciter.

12.2 Superposition as Coherence Indeterminacy

When a quantum system is in a superposition of states ∣A⟩+∣B⟩|A\rangle + |B\rangle∣A⟩+∣B⟩ relative to observer O, the framework reads this as: O’s coherence domain does not contain enough relational invariants to determine whether the system is in configuration A or configuration B. Both configurations are coherence-consistent with O’s current relational invariant structure.

This indeterminacy is ontic, not epistemic. It is not that the system has a definite value that O doesn’t know. It is that the system’s coherence state is genuinely underdetermined relative to O’s domain. No additional hidden variable resolves it — the underdetermination is in the relational structure itself.

At the same time, the complete coherence dependency graph — the atemporal block universe — is fully determinate. Every measurement outcome exists as a node in that graph. The superposition is a feature of O’s current projection of the complete graph, not of the graph itself. This reconciles quantum indeterminacy with the block universe without contradiction.

12.3 Epistemic Branching on a Single Ontic Topology

The framework denies Many Worlds-style ontic branching: the coherence geometry does not fork. There is one coherence dependency graph, complete and atemporal. But the framework permits — and requires — epistemic branching: the set of coherence-preserving projection paths available to an observer from any given node in the graph.

Before measurement, observer O is at a node from which multiple forward paths extend through the dependency graph: one path leads to the node where outcome A is registered; another leads to the node where outcome B is registered. Both paths are real paths in the complete graph. Both are coherence-preserving. Both represent genuine possible trajectories for O’s coherence domain.

After measurement, O’s coherence domain follows one path. The other path is not taken — it remains in the complete graph as an unrealized but real trajectory. It is not in another branch of reality. It is a coherence-preserving path that O’s projection did not traverse.

The branching is real — in the sense that multiple coherence-preserving forward paths genuinely exist — but it is epistemic rather than ontic. The graph does not split. O’s projection follows one path through a graph that always contained all paths.

12.4 The Born Rule Derived

The probability of outcome A — the Born rule weight ∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2|\langle A|\psi\rangle|^2∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2 — is the natural measure on epistemic branches induced by the coherence structure of the complete dependency graph.

The forward paths from O’s current node have coherence weights: the fraction of the total coherence content of all forward paths that flows through the path to outcome A. This weight is the coherence intensity of the projection of the current state onto ∣A⟩|A\rangle∣A⟩ — which is ∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2|\langle A|\psi\rangle|^2∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2. The Born rule is not postulated. It is the coherence-weighted measure on the set of coherence-preserving forward paths available from O’s current node.

The frequency interpretation of probability follows from this. An observer making repeated measurements traverses many nodes in the dependency graph. The fraction of traversals that reach outcome A converges to ∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2|\langle A|\psi\rangle|^2∣⟨A∣ψ⟩∣2 by the law of large numbers applied to coherence-weighted path traversal. The Born rule as an empirical frequency statement is the law of large numbers applied to the coherence geometry of the complete dependency graph.

12.5 The Preferred Basis

The preferred basis — the specific observable basis in which measurement produces definite outcomes — is fixed by the coherence-stability of forward projection paths. A measurement interaction generates a Type III relational invariant between the system and the measuring apparatus. The basis in which this relational invariant is diagonal — the basis in which the new relational invariant takes definite values — is the preferred basis.

This is the physical content of einselection: the environment selects the pointer basis by making certain states of the apparatus-system system more coherence-stable than others. Coherence-stable states are those in which the new relational invariant is consistent with the existing relational invariant structure of the apparatus and its environment. The preferred basis is the one that minimizes coherence disruption to the existing structure — the most coherence-stable forward path available from the current node.

12.6 The Single Outcome

The question ‘why this outcome rather than that one?’ is answered precisely by the epistemic branching structure. In the complete atemporal dependency graph, there is no randomness — every node exists, every path exists. O’s projection follows a specific path through this determined structure. The outcome that occurs is the node that O’s trajectory reaches.

From within O’s coherence domain — advancing through the graph node by node, unable to access the complete future structure — the forward paths appear genuinely open. The outcome appears random. From the perspective of the complete graph, every outcome is already a node, and every observer’s trajectory is already a path. Both perspectives are simultaneously correct at their respective levels of description.

Quantum randomness is the inside view of a determined atemporal structure, experienced from an observer whose coherence domain has not yet reached the relevant nodes. It is irreducibly real from within any bounded coherence domain, while being absent from the complete graph. This is not a paradox — it is the structural consequence of being an observer within the coherence network rather than outside it.

12.7 Wigner’s Friend

The Wigner’s friend thought experiment — in which an observer measures a quantum system, and a second observer then measures the first — is handled cleanly by the framework. Wigner’s friend, after measuring, has advanced her coherence domain to the node where the measurement relational invariant is established. She has a definite outcome relative to her domain.

Wigner, who has not yet interacted with the friend-system complex, is at an earlier node in the dependency graph. From his position, both forward paths — corresponding to the two possible measurement outcomes — are still open. His description of the friend-system complex as being in superposition is correct relative to his current projection.

Both descriptions are simultaneously correct relative to their respective nodes in the complete graph. There is no contradiction. When Wigner interacts with his friend — when his coherence domain advances to include the measurement node — his projection reaches the same node as his friend, and they agree on the outcome. Convergence upon interaction is guaranteed by the graph’s causal structure.

12.8 Comparison with Existing Interpretations

Copenhagen takes the wavefunction as epistemic and measurement as a primitive requiring no further analysis. The framework agrees that the wavefunction is relational but disagrees that measurement is primitive: it is a Type III interaction generating a relational invariant, fully analyzable in physical terms. The observer-classical boundary is not conventional in the framework — it is structural, defined by relational invariant density.

Many Worlds posits ontic branching: the wavefunction is real and universal, and all outcomes occur in non-communicating branches. The framework denies ontic branching — the coherence geometry does not fork — but preserves epistemic branching as the set of coherence-preserving forward paths in the single complete graph. The ontological extravagance of Many Worlds is avoided; the structural account of superposition and measurement is retained.

Bohmian mechanics posits definite particle positions at all times, with the wavefunction as a guiding field. The framework denies pre-existing definite values — the coherence state is genuinely underdetermined before crystallization. GRW objective collapse modifies quantum mechanics with new parameters. The framework predicts no modification to unitary evolution — unitarity is coherence conservation, which is absolute.

Relational quantum mechanics (Rovelli) is the closest existing interpretation: quantum states are relative to observers, and there are no observer-independent facts about quantum systems. The framework agrees on the relational structure but provides an ontic grounding — coherence conservation — from which the relational structure is derived rather than postulated. It also derives the Born rule, which relational QM does not.
