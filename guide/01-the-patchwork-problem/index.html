The Patchwork Problem

Why physics doesn't fit together — and what that might mean

Physics has four extraordinary frameworks, each spectacularly successful in its own domain. Why can't we make them fit together?

Questions this page addresses

  • Why are quantum mechanics and general relativity incompatible?
  • Why does the Standard Model have 19 free parameters?
  • Why has the measurement problem persisted for a century?
  • What role should observers play in physics?

Physics works. That is the remarkable fact that any honest attempt at a new framework has to begin with. Quantum mechanics predicts experimental outcomes to twelve decimal places. General relativity describes the bending of starlight, the precession of Mercury, and the rippling of spacetime from colliding black holes — all confirmed. Thermodynamics governs every engine, every chemical reaction, every star. The Standard Model catalogues the particles and forces with a precision that generations of experimentalists have been unable to fault.

And yet these frameworks do not fit together.

The Standard Story

Quantum mechanics describes the very small. General relativity describes the very large. Thermodynamics describes the flow of energy. The Standard Model describes particles and forces. Each is internally consistent, extraordinarily well-tested, and deeply understood. The problem is not that any of them is wrong. The problem is that they cannot all be right simultaneously, at least not in their current forms.

General relativity treats spacetime as a smooth, continuous fabric that curves in response to mass and energy. Quantum mechanics says that at small enough scales, nothing is smooth — everything fluctuates, superposes, and exists in probabilistic clouds. Ask what happens to spacetime itself at the Planck scale — about 10⁻³⁵ meters — and you get mathematical nonsense. The equations produce infinities that cannot be tamed by the techniques that work elsewhere.

The Standard Model, meanwhile, is a spectacular fit to the data — but it requires 19 free parameters: particle masses, coupling constants, mixing angles. These numbers are measured, not explained. Why does the electron have the mass it has? Why three generations of matter and not two or seven? Why this particular set of symmetry groups? The model catalogs the answers without explaining them.

Then there is the measurement problem. Quantum mechanics describes systems evolving according to the Schrödinger equation — smooth, deterministic, reversible. Until someone looks. Then, somehow, the smooth evolution is replaced by a sudden, random jump to a definite outcome. A century of interpretations — Copenhagen, Many Worlds, Bohmian mechanics, objective collapse — have not produced consensus on what, physically, is happening.

Underneath all of this sits a deeper puzzle: the role of the observer. In classical physics, observers are passive — they watch but do not participate. In quantum mechanics, the observer seems to matter, but nobody agrees on how or why. In general relativity, the observer is a coordinate system, chosen for convenience. In thermodynamics, entropy depends on what the observer can and cannot see. The observer is simultaneously everywhere in physics and nowhere in the foundations.

A Different Starting Point

Observer-Centrism asks a question that is either very simple or very ambitious, depending on your temperament: What if you started from scratch?

Not from quantum mechanics. Not from spacetime. Not from fields or particles or symmetries. Instead, from the most stripped-down question you can ask: What is the minimum set of assumptions from which the major structures of physics would have to follow?

The framework’s answer is three axioms — one about conservation, one about what it means to observe, and one about stability. From these, it claims to derive quantum mechanics, general relativity, thermodynamics, the particle spectrum, holography, and the basis of experience. Not by postulating them, but by showing that they are structurally necessary — that any universe with these three properties would inevitably develop these features.

That is a large claim. The rest of this guide is an attempt to make it concrete — to walk through the logic, show what follows from what, point to the formal derivations where the mathematics is rigorous, and be honest about where the framework is solid and where it is still developing.

On solid ground: The patchwork problem is real. The incompatibility between quantum mechanics and general relativity, the unexplained parameters of the Standard Model, and the measurement problem are all widely acknowledged open problems in physics. The ambition to address them from a unified starting point is shared by string theory, loop quantum gravity, and other programs. What distinguishes Observer-Centrism is the specific starting point: observers and coherence, rather than strings, loops, or extra dimensions.

Work in progress: Whether three axioms are truly sufficient to derive all of physics — or whether the framework smuggles in additional assumptions along the way — is exactly what the 28 rigorous derivations and 14 structural postulates on this site aim to make transparent. The honest answer is: mostly sufficient, with some additional motivated (but not derived) assumptions required at specific points.

The first step is to meet those three axioms. They are surprisingly compact.
