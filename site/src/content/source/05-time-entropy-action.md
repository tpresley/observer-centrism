## Time, Entropy, and Action

Three of the most fundamental concepts in physics — time, entropy, and action — are often treated as primitive or near-primitive. In Observer-Centrism, all three are derived from the axioms and the interaction structure of the observer network.

### 5.1 Time

Time, in the framework, is not a background parameter against which physical events unfold. It is the ordering structure forced by coherence-preserving interactions across the observer network.

Each minimal observer has an internal cycle — a loop that closes repeatedly, advancing phase with each traversal. When two observers interact via Type I passage, phase is transferred between them. This transfer is ordered: the phase advancement of one observer constrains the phase advancement of others it has interacted with. The accumulation of these constraints across the entire observer network generates a global ordering — a partial order on interaction events.

This partial order is time. It is derived from the phase structure of the observer network, not postulated as a background. 'Earlier' and 'later' are coherence-accessibility relations: event A is earlier than event B if A is a coherence ancestor of B in the dependency graph of interactions.

The arrow of time — the fact that time has a direction — follows immediately. Relational invariants, once generated by Type III interactions, are conserved. They cannot be un-generated. The process of relational invariant accumulation is irreversible, and the direction of this irreversibility is the arrow of time. Time flows in the direction of increasing relational invariant depth in the coherence dependency graph.

A universe with no observers would have no time — not merely in the epistemic sense that there would be nobody to notice, but in the structural sense that there would be no phase advancement, no interaction ordering, no dependency structure. Time is constituted by the observer network, not inhabited by it.

### 5.2 Entropy

Entropy in standard thermodynamics is a measure of disorder, or equivalently of missing information, or equivalently of the number of microscopic configurations consistent with a given macroscopic description. These formulations agree in practice but differ in their conceptual foundations.

In Observer-Centrism, entropy is precisely defined without ambiguity: the entropy of a system relative to observer A is the total coherence of the system minus the coherence accessible within A's coherence domain. Entropy is always observer-indexed — it is not a property of a system in isolation but of a system relative to a specific coherence boundary.

The second law of thermodynamics — entropy increases — follows directly from the bootstrap mechanism. Each Type III interaction generates relational invariants, some of which fall within A's coherence domain and some of which do not. Those that fall outside are, by definition, inaccessible to A — they increase A's entropy. Since interactions continuously generate relational invariants, and only some fraction of these fall within any given observer's domain, entropy increases for every bounded observer over time. The second law is not a statistical tendency — it is a structural consequence of coherence conservation in a network of bounded observers.

Maximum entropy for a given observer is not universal heat death. It is the saturation of that observer's coherence integration capacity — the state in which no further relational invariants generated by interactions fall within the observer's domain. Other observers, with different coherence domains, may simultaneously be accumulating accessible structure. The global entropy of the universe — relative to the universe as its own observer — is constant: total coherence is conserved.

### 5.3 Action and the Quantum of Coherence

Action, in classical mechanics, is the time-integral of the Lagrangian — a quantity minimized along physical trajectories by the principle of stationary action. In quantum mechanics, action appears in the path integral, where each trajectory is weighted by a phase factor involving the action divided by Planck's constant $\hbar$.

In the framework, action is the coherence cost of a transformation. Every transformation that an observer undergoes — every traversal of its internal loop, every interaction with another observer — has a coherence cost: the minimum amount of coherence required to execute the transformation while maintaining the observer's invariant.

The principle of stationary action is then a coherence selection rule: physical paths are those in exact resonance with the global coherence structure. Paths that deviate from the stationary action condition are coherence-dissonant — they require more than the minimum coherence cost and are therefore disfavored by coherence conservation. The least-action path is the most coherence-efficient path.

Planck's constant $\hbar$ has a precise meaning in this framework: it is the coherence cost of one minimal observer cycle. This is the irreducible quantum of action — the minimum cost of any coherence-preserving transformation, set by the structure of the most primitive observer loop. $\hbar$ is not an empirical free parameter. It is the unit of coherence cost, fixed by the normalization of the minimal observer cycle.

The uncertainty principle — that position and momentum cannot be simultaneously specified to arbitrary precision — follows from this. The phase position of an observer's loop and the cycle count of that loop are conjugate aspects of the coherence unit: knowing one precisely makes the other indeterminate. This is not a limitation of measurement technology. It is the structure of the coherence quantum itself.

The path integral formulation of quantum mechanics — summing over all paths weighted by coherence phase — is the coherence resonance selection mechanism operating across the full observer network. Paths interfere constructively when their coherence phases align; they interfere destructively when they cancel. The physical trajectory emerges as the coherence-resonant result of this selection.
