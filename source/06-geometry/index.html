6. Geometry from Observers

Geometry from Observers

The framework derives spacetime geometry from observer structure rather than postulating it. The key is the loop closure condition applied to the relationship between spatial extent and temporal duration — and from there, to the full structure of relativistic spacetime and gravity.

6.1 Rods, Clocks, and the Speed of Light

A clock, in the framework, is an observer whose phase advancement is readable as a relational invariant by other observers — a loop whose cycle count can be compared with other loops. A rod is an observer that encodes coherence distance: the number of interaction steps between two points in the observer network.

These two descriptions are not independent. The loop closure condition requires that the spatial extent of an observer’s loop and the temporal duration of its cycle satisfy a fixed relationship: the loop must close in both space and time simultaneously. For a loop of spatial extent LLL and temporal period TTT, the closure condition requires L=cTL = cTL=cT, where ccc is the rate of phase propagation through the coherence geometry.

The speed of light ccc is therefore not an empirical constant requiring measurement. It is the universal phase propagation speed — the rate at which coherence advances through the observer network — fixed by the loop closure condition. A rod of length LLL is structurally identical to a clock of period T=L/cT = L/cT=L/c. Space and time are not independent: they are two aspects of the single loop closure geometry, related by ccc.

Lorentz contraction and time dilation — the apparent shortening of moving rods and slowing of moving clocks — are not separate effects. They are one effect: a loop tilted in the coherence geometry projects differently onto the spatial and temporal axes of any given observer. The Minkowski metric — the geometry of special relativity — is the coherence geometry of minimal observer loops. It is not postulated but derived from the loop closure condition in the coherence network.

6.2 Gravity as Coherence Geometry Curvature

A massive observer — a composite structure with many levels of relational invariants — generates a density gradient in the surrounding coherence geometry. The relational invariants constituting a massive observer do not terminate at its surface; they extend outward, becoming sparser with distance. This gradient is the gravitational field.

The gradient modifies the local loop closure condition for any observer in the vicinity. Loops closing near a massive object are assisted by the higher density of relational invariants — their closure is energetically favored in that direction. This is the gravitational attraction: not a force pulling observers toward massive objects, but a coherence-cost gradient that makes loop closure easier in the direction of higher relational invariant density. Observers follow the path of minimum coherence cost, which is the geodesic of the curved geometry.

The equivalence principle — the observation that gravitational and inertial mass are identical, that free-fall is locally indistinguishable from the absence of gravity — is an immediate consequence. The geodesic is determined by the local coherence geometry alone, independent of the observer’s internal structure. Every observer, regardless of its mass or composition, follows the same geodesic in the same coherence geometry.

The Einstein field equations — the equations that relate the curvature of spacetime to the distribution of energy and momentum — are, in the framework, the global coherence consistency conditions on the geometry. They express the requirement that the coherence geometry be globally consistent: that the curvature generated by observers be consistent with the trajectories those observers follow, which is consistent with the curvature, in a self-referential loop that has exactly one consistent solution for given initial data. The Einstein equations are not laws imposed on a pre-existing geometry — they are the fixed-point conditions of the geometry’s self-consistency.

6.3 Gravitational Waves and the Planck Scale

When two large composite observers undergo a Type II fusion event — a black hole merger, for example — the relational invariant density field of the combined system reorganizes rapidly. This reorganization propagates outward through the coherence geometry at the speed of phase propagation ccc. The propagating reorganization is a gravitational wave: a physical ripple in the coherence geometry, carrying information about the merger to distant observers.

The Planck length ℓP\ell_PℓP​ and Planck time tPt_PtP​ mark the scale at which the continuum approximation to the coherence geometry fails. At or below these scales, the discrete network of relational invariants becomes individually visible. The smooth differentiable manifold of general relativity is the large-scale average of this discrete structure — a valid approximation at scales large compared to ℓP\ell_PℓP​, but not at the Planck scale itself.

The fundamental constants GGG (Newton’s gravitational constant), ℏ\hbarℏ, and ccc are all expressions of the coherence geometry rather than independent empirical parameters. They set the unit of coherence cost (ℏ\hbarℏ), the unit of phase propagation (ccc), and the strength of coupling between relational invariant density and geometry (GGG). Whether GGG is fully derivable from ℏ\hbarℏ and ccc, or whether it constitutes an additional free parameter of the framework, remains an open question.
