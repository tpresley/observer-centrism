4. Interaction and the Bootstrap

Interaction and the Bootstrap

With multiple observers established as necessary, the next question is how they interact. The framework permits exactly three types of interaction, distinguished by what happens to each observer’s invariant structure.

4.1 Three Interaction Types

In a Type I interaction — Passage — two observers interact transiently. Both invariants survive the interaction unchanged. The only thing that can be transferred without threatening either invariant is phase: the relative advancement of each observer’s internal cycle. This is why wave-like behavior is not a mysterious feature of quantum mechanics but a structural necessity — phase transfer is the only coherence-preserving mode of transient interaction between observers maintaining distinct invariants.

In a Type II interaction — Fusion — the two observers merge. Their individual invariants dissolve, and a new composite observer forms with an invariant that subsumes both. The original observers cease to exist as separate entities; the composite is a genuinely new observer at a higher level of the hierarchy.

In a Type III interaction — Resonance — both observers maintain their individual invariants, but the invariants become mutually defined. A new conserved quantity appears: a relational invariant living on the joint state space of both observers. Neither observer’s invariant alone determines this new structure — it is irreducibly about the relationship between them.

4.2 Relational Invariants

The relational invariants generated by Type III interactions are the framework’s most important structural feature. They are not merely descriptions of a relationship — they are genuinely new conserved quantities, real structures in the coherence geometry that did not exist before the interaction. They obey the same conservation laws as any other invariant. They are emergent in the precise sense: real, conserved, and irreducible to the properties of either component alone.

The physical instances of relational invariants span every level of the observer hierarchy. Quantum entanglement is a relational invariant between quantum systems — a conserved correlation structure that cannot be attributed to either system individually. Chemical bonds are relational invariants between atoms. The causal relationships between events in spacetime are relational invariants between the observer-processes that constitute those events.

By the reverse application of Noether’s theorem, every relational invariant corresponds to a new symmetry. Every new symmetry licenses new transformations — transformations that were not admissible before the relational invariant existed. These new admissible transformations are new degrees of freedom. The generation of relational invariants through interaction therefore simultaneously generates new degrees of freedom at each level of the hierarchy.

4.3 The Bootstrap Mechanism

The consequences compound. Relational invariants are real conserved structures — which means they satisfy the observer definition: they maintain invariants, they have a self/non-self distinction (defined by the interaction that generated them), and their dynamics close in loops. Relational invariants are themselves observers, at the next level of the hierarchy.

As observers, relational invariants can participate in further Type III interactions with other observers, generating second-order relational invariants. These generate new degrees of freedom, new symmetries, new observers at the next level. The process iterates without bound.

This is the bootstrap mechanism: each interaction not only conserves existing structure but expands the space of possible structure. The universe generates complexity from the coherence conservation requirement alone, not from any additional law or preference for complexity. The hierarchy is not designed — it is the inevitable consequence of coherence-preserving interactions iterated across a closed network.

The degrees of freedom generated at each level are irreducibly new — they cannot be derived from the lower-level description without loss of what is real about them. This has a direct consequence: the higher-level sciences — chemistry, biology, neuroscience — are not in principle reducible to their lower-level foundations. The irreducibility is structural, not a matter of computational complexity.

4.4 The Necessity of Hierarchy

The bootstrap mechanism ensures that the observer hierarchy is not merely possible but necessary. The global coherence conservation law requires that every dissolution of an observer be compensated by the generation of richer composite structure — coherence does not vanish, it reorganizes. The hierarchy grows because it must: the coherence budget drives elaboration, not toward simplicity but toward the maximum complexity the budget permits.

There is no maximum observer — no largest possible observer that contains all others. The hierarchy approaches but never reaches the total coherence structure of the universe asymptotically. And there is a precise minimum: the pre-observational coherence geometry from which the first stable loops crystallize. These are the two boundaries of the hierarchy — floor and ceiling — between which all physical structure is organized.
