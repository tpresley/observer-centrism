## Introduction

Physics has accumulated an extraordinary collection of successful theories. Quantum mechanics predicts experimental outcomes to more decimal places than any other framework in the history of science. General relativity describes gravity and the large-scale structure of spacetime with matching precision. Thermodynamics governs the flow of energy across every scale we can probe. The Standard Model catalogues the elementary particles and their interactions with remarkable completeness.

And yet these frameworks do not fit together. Quantum mechanics and general relativity are mathematically incompatible at the Planck scale. The Standard Model contains nineteen free parameters — masses, coupling constants, mixing angles — that are measured but not explained. Thermodynamics is derived, under certain assumptions, from statistical mechanics, but the origin of the arrow of time remains contested. The measurement problem in quantum mechanics — why experiments yield definite outcomes from indefinite quantum states — has generated interpretational debates for a century without resolution. The relationship between physical law and the observers who discover those laws is either ignored or treated as philosophically embarrassing.

Observer-Centrism begins from a different starting point. Rather than taking any of these frameworks as foundational and attempting to extend it, we ask: what is the minimum set of primitive commitments from which the major structures of physics can be derived? The answer turns out to be surprisingly compact — three axioms and one structural observation — and the structures that follow from them include not only quantum mechanics and gravity but the statistical structure of the particle spectrum, the necessity of holography, and a principled account of measurement.

The central move is to take observers seriously as physical entities defined by a precise structural criterion, and to take seriously the constraint that whatever is real must be conserved. From these two commitments, together with the requirement that observers be dynamically stable, a rich structure emerges — not by assumption but by necessity.

This document introduces the framework, traces the major derivations, and identifies where it makes predictions that differ from current theoretical expectations. The treatment is intended to be rigorous in structure without being heavy in formalism. The goal is to make the logical progression clear enough that a careful reader can assess the framework's validity and scope.

A note on organization: the document proceeds from the most fundamental level — the axioms and minimal observer structure — up through geometry, quantum mechanics, the particle spectrum, and holography. A final section addresses the framework's implications for questions of mind and experience. This material is placed deliberately at the end and can be read independently, because it engages questions that some readers will regard as outside physics proper. The physical content of the framework does not depend on it.
