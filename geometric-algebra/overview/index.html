Geometric Algebra Exploration

provisional Cl(p,q) high priority

What is Geometric Algebra?

Geometric Algebra (GA), also called Clifford algebra, is the algebra generated by a vector space equipped with a quadratic form. Given a vector space VVV with quadratic form QQQ, the Clifford algebra Cl⁡(V,Q)\operatorname{Cl}(V, Q)Cl(V,Q) is the associative algebra generated by VVV subject to the relation

v2=Q(v)for all v∈V.v^2 = Q(v) \quad \text{for all } v \in V.v2=Q(v)for all v∈V.

For a real vector space with signature (p,q)(p, q)(p,q), where ppp dimensions square to +1+1+1 and qqq dimensions square to −1-1−1, we write Cl⁡(p,q)\operatorname{Cl}(p,q)Cl(p,q). This single construction unifies scalars, vectors, bivectors, and higher-grade objects into one algebra with one product — the geometric product.

GA’s power lies in encoding geometric operations (rotations, reflections, projections) as algebraic operations with a single product, replacing the patchwork of cross products, dot products, matrix representations, and component gymnastics found in conventional formulations.

Why Explore GA for This Framework?

The framework already makes extensive use of the division algebras R,C,H,O\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}, \mathbb{O}R,C,H,O through the bootstrap-division-algebras derivation. These division algebras have deep connections to Clifford algebras:

  • Quaternions: H≅Cl⁡+(3,0)\mathbb{H} \cong \operatorname{Cl}^+(3,0)H≅Cl+(3,0), the even subalgebra of three-dimensional Euclidean Clifford algebra.
  • Complexified octonions: C⊗O≅Cl⁡(6)\mathbb{C} \otimes \mathbb{O} \cong \operatorname{Cl}(6)C⊗O≅Cl(6), already referenced in the anomaly-cancellation and standard-model-group derivations.
  • Complex numbers: C≅Cl⁡+(2,0)≅Cl⁡(0,1)\mathbb{C} \cong \operatorname{Cl}^+(2,0) \cong \operatorname{Cl}(0,1)C≅Cl+(2,0)≅Cl(0,1), the simplest non-trivial case.

Several core derivations — Lorentz invariance, spin-statistics, CPT theorem, electromagnetism, chirality selection, weak interaction — use mathematical structures that have particularly clean GA formulations. The question is whether recasting these derivations in GA language reveals additional structure, simplifies proofs, or provides pedagogical clarity beyond what the current formulations achieve.

Assessment Methodology

Each candidate derivation was evaluated on three criteria:

  1. Natural translation: Does the existing mathematics have a straightforward GA formulation, or would the translation be forced?
  2. Genuine insight: Does GA provide structural understanding beyond a change of notation? Does it unify steps, eliminate auxiliary constructions, or reveal hidden connections?
  3. Proof simplification: Does GA shorten or clarify proofs? Does it make implicit structure explicit?

Candidates were classified as:

  • High priority: Strong positive signals on at least two of the three criteria. These are the candidates most likely to yield genuine insight.
  • Moderate priority: Positive on one criterion, neutral on others. Worth exploring but less certain to yield results.
  • Low priority: Primarily notational translation with limited structural payoff.

  • Lorentz invariance: The Lorentz group is the rotor group of Cl⁡(1,3)\operatorname{Cl}(1,3)Cl(1,3). Boosts and rotations are unified as R=e−Bθ/2R = e^{-B\theta/2}R=e−Bθ/2 with the bivector BBB selecting the transformation plane. Double cover is manifest.
  • CPT theorem: Discrete symmetries CCC, PPP, TTT are grade automorphisms of the Clifford algebra. CPT == = pseudoscalar multiplication. The theorem follows from the structure of Cl⁡(1,3)\operatorname{Cl}(1,3)Cl(1,3).
  • Chirality selection: Left/right chirality is γ5\gamma_5γ5​ grading, which in STA is multiplication by III. The chiral projectors 12(1±γ5)\frac{1}{2}(1 \pm \gamma_5)21​(1±γ5​) select idempotent subalgebras.
  • Spin-statistics: The 4π4\pi4π periodicity of half-integer spin follows from R(θ)=e−Bθ/2R(\theta) = e^{-B\theta/2}R(θ)=e−Bθ/2 — a 2π2\pi2π rotation gives R=−1R = -1R=−1, requiring two full turns to return to +1+1+1.

Relationship to Division Algebras

The Cayley-Dickson doubling sequence and the Clifford algebra construction both build larger algebras from smaller ones, but they differ in a crucial way:

Cayley-Dickson doubling produces:

R→C→H→O→S→⋯\mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{C} \to \mathbb{H} \to \mathbb{O} \to \mathbb{S} \to \cdotsR→C→H→O→S→⋯

Each step doubles the dimension but progressively loses algebraic properties: C\mathbb{C}C loses ordering, H\mathbb{H}H loses commutativity, O\mathbb{O}O loses associativity, and S\mathbb{S}S (sedenions) loses the division property entirely.

Clifford algebra construction produces algebras Cl⁡(n)\operatorname{Cl}(n)Cl(n) of dimension 2n2^n2n that are always associative. They never lose associativity regardless of dimension. Instead, they develop richer internal structure (more grades, more complex center).

The key isomorphisms connecting these two sequences are:

H≅Cl⁡+(3,0),C⊗O≅Cl⁡(6).\mathbb{H} \cong \operatorname{Cl}^+(3,0), \qquad \mathbb{C} \otimes \mathbb{O} \cong \operatorname{Cl}(6).H≅Cl+(3,0),C⊗O≅Cl(6).

The first isomorphism explains why the weak interaction’s SU(2)\mathrm{SU}(2)SU(2) gauge group is simultaneously describable via quaternions and via rotors. The second explains why the full Standard Model gauge structure fits inside a single 64-dimensional Clifford algebra.

The divergence between these two constructions — Cayley-Dickson loses associativity, Clifford never does — illuminates the gauge hierarchy termination. The framework’s bootstrap-division-algebras derivation shows that the bootstrap mechanism forces Cayley-Dickson doubling (because each level requires genuinely new algebraic structure), and the termination at O\mathbb{O}O (because sedenion zero divisors violate coherence conservation) is precisely the point where the associative Clifford description and the non-associative Cayley-Dickson description permanently diverge.

Key GA Concepts

For reference, the essential GA definitions used throughout this section:

Geometric product. For vectors a,b∈Va, b \in Va,b∈V, the geometric product is:

ab=a⋅b+a∧bab = a \cdot b + a \wedge bab=a⋅b+a∧b

where a⋅b=12(ab+ba)a \cdot b = \tfrac{1}{2}(ab + ba)a⋅b=21​(ab+ba) is the symmetric (inner) part and a∧b=12(ab−ba)a \wedge b = \tfrac{1}{2}(ab - ba)a∧b=21​(ab−ba) is the antisymmetric (outer/wedge) part. The geometric product is associative but generally non-commutative.

Grade. A kkk-blade is the wedge product of kkk vectors. A kkk-vector (grade-kkk element) is a linear combination of kkk-blades. In Cl⁡(n)\operatorname{Cl}(n)Cl(n), grades range from 000 (scalars) to nnn (pseudoscalars). The grade-kkk part of a multivector MMM is written ⟨M⟩k\langle M \rangle_k⟨M⟩k​.

Bivector. A grade-2 element, representing an oriented plane. Bivectors generate rotations: a bivector BBB with B2=−∣B∣2B^2 = -|B|^2B2=−∣B∣2 generates a rotation in the plane of BBB. In Cl⁡(3,0)\operatorname{Cl}(3,0)Cl(3,0), the basis bivectors are e23,e31,e12e_{23}, e_{31}, e_{12}e23​,e31​,e12​.

Rotor. An even-grade element RRR satisfying RR~=1R\tilde{R} = 1RR~=1, where R~\tilde{R}R~ denotes the reverse. Rotors implement rotations via the sandwich product v↦RvR~v \mapsto Rv\tilde{R}v↦RvR~. Every rotor can be written as R=e−Bθ/2R = e^{-B\theta/2}R=e−Bθ/2 for some unit bivector BBB and angle θ\thetaθ.

Pseudoscalar. The highest-grade element, the product of all basis vectors. In Cl⁡(3,0)\operatorname{Cl}(3,0)Cl(3,0): I=e123I = e_{123}I=e123​ with I2=−1I^2 = -1I2=−1. In Cl⁡(1,3)\operatorname{Cl}(1,3)Cl(1,3): I=e0123I = e_{0123}I=e0123​ with I2=−1I^2 = -1I2=−1 (signature-dependent). The pseudoscalar plays a central role in duality and chirality.

Reversion. The anti-automorphism M~\tilde{M}M~ that reverses the order of all vector factors: ab~=ba\widetilde{ab} = baab=ba, abc~=cba\widetilde{abc} = cbaabc=cba. For a grade-kkk element, M~=(−1)k(k−1)/2M\tilde{M} = (-1)^{k(k-1)/2} MM~=(−1)k(k−1)/2M. Reversion is the GA analog of Hermitian conjugation.

Multivector. A general element of the Clifford algebra, a sum of elements of different grades. Every element of Cl⁡(p,q)\operatorname{Cl}(p,q)Cl(p,q) is a multivector. Physical quantities are typically of definite grade (scalars, vectors, bivectors), but operators and transformations are generally mixed-grade.
