## Framing

This note describes a small research program that takes the observer as the primitive object — formally defined as a triple $\mathcal{O} = (\Sigma, I, \mathcal{B})$ consisting of a state space, a conserved invariant, and a self/non-self partition of the group of state-preserving automorphisms — and derives the discrete causal substrate as a consequence rather than postulating it. The program shares the causal-set / emergent-Lorentzian layer with WPP but enters it from the opposite direction: no rewrite rule is chosen, because the combinatorial network is forced by a multiplicity theorem on observers.

The purpose of this note is not to argue the program is correct. It is to surface four technical objects — three theorems, two open problems, and one falsifiable prediction — that I think a WPP researcher working on the categorical and geometric emergence side would find worth poking at.

## Three theorems illustrating the style

**1. Born rule from a Cauchy multiplicative functional equation.** Let $F: \mathcal{H} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ be a continuous functional on quantum states satisfying (i) $U(1)$ phase invariance, (ii) additivity across independent channels, (iii) composition-compatibility under tensor products, and (iv) non-triviality. The unique solution is $F(\psi) = \langle \psi | \psi \rangle$. The exponent $\alpha = 1$ in the general power-law family $f(r) = r^\alpha$ is forced by the composition constraint via Cauchy's multiplicative equation. Gleason's theorem gives an independent route; this one is constructive and does not require dimension $\geq 3$.

**2. Generation counting from $\dim \mathfrak{so}(3)$.** Fermions are modelled as observer loops carrying winding classes in the rotation group of the internal configuration space. The strong-subadditivity condition on an entropy-like coherence functional, applied to the internal winding-direction space, forces at least three independent sectors. The Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(3)$ admits at most three independent generators. Exactly three. The CKM mechanism (Kobayashi–Maskawa) and CP violation then follow from the usual three-generation algebra. The novelty is that the generation count is a theorem about the internal space, not an input.

**3. Division algebra selection from a normed composition identity.** The classification of admissible gauge structures reduces to the Hurwitz theorem via an identification between the norm on the algebra and the entropy-like coherence functional: unit elements act as isometries because coherence is conserved, which forces the composition identity $\|xy\| = \|x\|\|y\|$, which forces $\mathbb{R}, \mathbb{C}, \mathbb{H}, \mathbb{O}$. The cleanliness here is that "why Hurwitz at all?" gets a structural answer rather than being postulated.

The program currently has 26 derivations whose central claims are independent of all remaining structural postulates, against 44 at the "rigorous given auxiliary postulates" tier. A postulate audit (five irreducible postulates remaining, fifteen previously-postulated items now theorems out of twenty total) is documented and explicit. I mention counts only to indicate that the epistemology is maintained seriously; the individual results are what matter.

## Two open problems that may interest you directly

**Problem A: the reflexive domain equation $\mathcal{U} \cong \mathcal{R}(\mathcal{U}, \mathcal{U})$.** The self-consistency requirement for the framework reduces to a Scott-style reflexive fixed-point equation in a category of observers-with-morphisms. The bootstrap map $\mathcal{R}$ takes pairs of observers to the observer generated by their interaction's conserved quantity; the framework requires a universe-object $\mathcal{U}$ that is its own bootstrap image. The ingredients needed for a proof are: (i) Scott-continuity of $\mathcal{R}$, (ii) an enrichment of the observer category to one where reflexive domain equations have solutions, (iii) a contraction or compactness argument under the coherence ceiling. Dana Scott's 1972 construction is the obvious template. Uniqueness of the fixed point, if established, would promote the area-scaling postulate to a theorem and — via the geometric realization — fix the cosmological constant.

**Problem B: a partial geometry functor $G: \mathbf{Obs} \to \mathbf{Geom}$.** The framework has a partial construction that sends each observer to an effective Lorentzian geometry encoding its epistemic horizon, with cross-level consistency conditions compatible with the bootstrap map $\mathcal{R}$. The full functor is not yet constructed; what exists is a hierarchical sequence of effective metrics indexed by bootstrap level and a conjecture that functoriality across levels constrains the cosmological constant to a specific value at the fixed point. This is close enough to the categorical side of WPP's GR-emergence work that I suspect tools you have developed for hypergraph-indexed metrics could be imported or adapted.

## Bridge to causal-graph programs

The derivation chain passes through a labelled causal set $(C, \prec, \lambda)$ with Planck-density elements and a Lorentzian manifold as continuum limit. This is the same object WPP exits into above the rewrite layer. The difference is upstream: $C$ is derived as the interaction record of a network of observers (with a theorem that the network must be boundaryless because strong subadditivity becomes vacuous at an edge), rather than as the causal graph of a chosen rewrite rule. Above this layer, I expect the two frameworks' machinery for dimension emergence, causal-invariance-to-Lorentz-invariance, and horizon structure to be largely interchangeable. Sorkin–Bombelli–Dowker causal set theory is the natural shared language.

## One falsifiable prediction

A discrete causal substrate at the Planck scale induces an irreducible single-arm strain power spectral density $S_h = \alpha_H \ell_P / c$ in interferometers, with $\alpha_H \lesssim 0.5$ from Holometer. The distinctive content is not the amplitude — which coincides with Hogan-type models — but the *angular structure*. For two Michelson interferometers at relative orientation $\beta$, the overlap reduction function is $\Gamma(\beta) = \cos\beta$ (derived, not assumed). The predicted cross-correlation ratio $S_{12}(0^\circ) / S_{12}(45^\circ) = \sqrt{2}$ and the null $S_{12}(90^\circ) = 0$ between perpendicular co-located Michelsons distinguish this framework from isotropic GWB models, from Hogan's model, and from null hypotheses. The experimental geometry is a rotatable pair of co-located interferometers, which is a tractable instrument design. LIGO H–L is specifically a poor test, because the detectors are near-perpendicular and separated — $\Gamma \approx 0$ — which is why existing searches do not constrain this signature.
