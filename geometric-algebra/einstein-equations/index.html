Target: Einstein Field Equations as Fixed-Point Conditions (status: rigorous)

The target derivation proves that the Einstein field equations Gμν+Λgμν=(8πG/c4)TμνG_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = (8\pi G/c^4) T_{\mu\nu}Gμν​+Λgμν​=(8πG/c4)Tμν​ are the unique self-consistency conditions of the coherence geometry. The proof chains three constraints — tensorial structure, covariant conservation (from Axiom 1), and second-order locality (Structural Postulate S1) — through Lovelock’s uniqueness theorem (1971) to select the Einstein equations uniquely in 3+13+13+1 dimensions.

In STA and Gauge Theory Gravity (building on Gravity in STA), the entire apparatus of index-laden tensor calculus is replaced by linear functions operating on vectors and bivectors. The Riemann tensor becomes a bivector-to-bivector map R(B)\mathcal{R}(B)R(B). The Einstein tensor becomes a vector function G(a)\mathcal{G}(a)G(a). The field equations reduce to a single vector equation G(a)+Λa=κT(a)\mathcal{G}(a) + \Lambda a = \kappa \mathcal{T}(a)G(a)+Λa=κT(a). The Bianchi identity and covariant conservation become statements about the divergence of vector-valued functions — no Christoffel symbols, no index manipulation.

Step 1: The Riemann Bivector Map

From Gravity in STA (Step 3), the curvature in GTG is encoded as a bivector-valued function. We now develop this systematically.

Definition 1.1 (Riemann bivector map). The Riemann curvature R(B)\mathcal{R}(B)R(B) is a linear function from bivectors to bivectors:

R:⋀2→⋀2\mathcal{R}: \textstyle\bigwedge^2 \to \textstyle\bigwedge^2R:⋀2→⋀2

  • Self-adjointness: ⟨A R(B)⟩0=⟨R(A) B⟩0\langle A \, \mathcal{R}(B)\rangle_0 = \langle \mathcal{R}(A) \, B\rangle_0⟨AR(B)⟩0​=⟨R(A)B⟩0​ for all bivectors A,BA, BA,B (where ⟨⋅⟩0\langle\cdot\rangle_0⟨⋅⟩0​ is the scalar part). The two descriptions have the same information content, but R\mathcal{R}R is a single linear function while RμνρσR^\mu{}_{\nu\rho\sigma}Rμνρσ​ is a four-index array.

Step 4: The Bianchi Identity in STA

Theorem 4.1 (Bianchi identity as divergence-freedom). The Einstein function is covariantly divergence-free:

∇˙⋅G˙(a)=0\dot{\nabla} \cdot \dot{\mathcal{G}}(a) = 0∇˙⋅G˙​(a)=0

where the overdot notation indicates that the covariant derivative ∇\nabla∇ acts only on G\mathcal{G}G, not on the argument aaa (Doran-Lasenby convention for scope). This is the STA form of ∇μGμν=0\nabla_\mu G^{\mu\nu} = 0∇μ​Gμν=0.

Proof. The contracted Bianchi identity ∇μGμν=0\nabla_\mu G^{\mu\nu} = 0∇μ​Gμν=0 follows from the second Bianchi identity ∇[λRμν]ρσ=0\nabla_{[\lambda}R_{\mu\nu]\rho\sigma} = 0∇[λ​Rμν]ρσ​=0, which itself is a consequence of the Jacobi identity for covariant derivatives. (the covariant exterior derivative of the Riemann map vanishes). Contracting gives the divergence-freedom of G\mathcal{G}G. The computation is structurally identical to the standard proof but uses the STA derivative operator ∇\nabla∇ instead of index-based covariant derivatives. □\square□

Remark. The Bianchi identity ∇˙⋅G˙(a)=0\dot{\nabla} \cdot \dot{\mathcal{G}}(a) = 0∇˙⋅G˙​(a)=0 is a geometric identity — it holds for any metric, not just solutions of the Einstein equations. Proof. This is Lovelock’s theorem (1971) restated in STA notation. The standard proof classifies all symmetric divergence-free rank-2 tensors built from ggg and ∂2g\partial^2 g∂2g in 4 dimensions.
